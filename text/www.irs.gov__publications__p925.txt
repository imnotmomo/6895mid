Publication 925, Passive Activity and At-Risk Rules.

This document summarizes IRS guidance on passive activity limits and at-risk rules for individuals and certain other taxpayers.

Core ordering rule:
Before applying passive activity limits, first determine the amount of deductions disallowed under basis rules or at-risk rules. Passive activity limits are applied after those limits.

Who must use the passive activity rules:
The passive activity rules generally apply to individuals, estates, trusts other than grantor trusts, personal service corporations, and closely held corporations. Partnerships and S corporations do not apply the passive loss limits at the entity level; the rules generally apply at the owner level.

Passive activity loss:
A passive activity loss is generally not allowed for the year except to the extent of passive activity income, subject to special rules and allowances.

Rental activity rule:
A rental activity is generally passive even if the taxpayer materially participates, unless an exception applies such as real estate professional treatment or another specific exception.

Special allowance for rental real estate with active participation:
Some or all losses may be allowed under the special $25,000 allowance for certain rental real estate activities when the taxpayer actively participates, subject to phaseout based on income.

Material participation:
Trade or business activities are not passive if the taxpayer materially participates. Material participation is determined under the tests in the publication, including hour-based tests and other factual tests.

Real estate professional:
Rental real estate activities may avoid passive treatment if the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional and materially participates.

At-risk amounts:
A taxpayer is at risk in an activity for money contributed, the adjusted basis of property contributed, and certain borrowed amounts used in the activity.

Borrowed amounts included in amount at risk:
A taxpayer is at risk for borrowed amounts if the taxpayer is personally liable for repayment. A taxpayer may also be at risk for certain borrowing secured by property other than property used in the activity, limited by the net fair market value of the pledged property.

Amounts not at risk:
Nonrecourse financing and arrangements that protect against loss may reduce or eliminate the amount at risk, unless a special rule applies.

Loss disallowance under at-risk rules:
A loss from an activity is allowed only to the extent the taxpayer is at risk in the activity. Losses exceeding the amount at risk are suspended.

Later-year effect:
If the taxpayer increases the amount at risk in a later year, previously suspended at-risk losses may become deductible, but they can still be limited by the passive activity rules.

Disposition rule:
When there is a fully taxable disposition of the entire interest in a passive activity to an unrelated party, suspended passive losses are generally freed up under the rules described in the publication.

Useful decision sequence for individual-tax questions:
1. Determine adjusted basis and any basis limitation.
2. Determine the taxpayer's amount at risk.
3. Disallow any loss above the amount at risk.
4. Apply passive activity rules to the remaining deductible amount.
5. Apply any special rental real estate allowance, phaseout, or full-disposition rule.
