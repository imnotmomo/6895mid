IRS Publication 925 – Passive Activity and At-Risk Rules (Full Reference)

OVERVIEW
Publication 925 covers the passive activity loss rules under IRC Section 469 and the at-risk rules under IRC Section 465. These rules limit the amount of losses and credits a taxpayer can claim from passive activities and activities in which the taxpayer is not at risk.

PART I: PASSIVE ACTIVITY RULES

Definition of Passive Activity
A passive activity is any trade or business activity in which the taxpayer does not materially participate during the tax year, or any rental activity (with certain exceptions). Material participation means involvement in the operations of an activity on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis.

Material Participation Tests
A taxpayer materially participates in an activity if any ONE of the following seven tests is met:
1. The taxpayer participates in the activity for more than 500 hours during the tax year.
2. The taxpayer's participation in the activity constitutes substantially all of the participation in such activity of all individuals (including non-owners) for the tax year.
3. The taxpayer participates in the activity for more than 100 hours during the tax year, and no other individual participates more than the taxpayer.
4. The activity is a significant participation activity (more than 100 hours), and the taxpayer's total participation in all significant participation activities exceeds 500 hours.
5. The taxpayer materially participated in the activity for any 5 of the prior 10 tax years.
6. The activity is a personal service activity, and the taxpayer materially participated for any 3 prior tax years.
7. Based on all facts and circumstances, the taxpayer participates on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis.

Passive Activity Loss (PAL) Rules
Losses from passive activities can generally only be deducted against income from passive activities. Passive activity losses cannot offset active income (such as wages, salaries, and business income from activities in which the taxpayer materially participates) or portfolio income (such as interest, dividends, and capital gains from investments). Disallowed passive losses are suspended and carried forward to future years. When the taxpayer disposes of the entire interest in the passive activity in a fully taxable transaction to an unrelated party, all suspended passive losses from that activity are fully deductible.

Passive Activity Income
Passive activity income includes income from passive activities such as rental income, income from a trade or business in which the taxpayer does not materially participate, and gain from the disposition of an interest in a passive activity.

Portfolio Income Is Not Passive
Portfolio income is not treated as passive activity income. Portfolio income includes interest, dividends, royalties (not derived in the ordinary course of a trade or business), annuities, and gain or loss from the disposition of property held for investment.

Rental Activities
Rental activities are generally considered passive activities regardless of whether the taxpayer materially participates. However, there are exceptions:
- The average rental period is 7 days or less.
- The average rental period is 30 days or less and significant personal services are provided.
- Extraordinary personal services are provided.
- The rental is incidental to a nonrental activity.
- The taxpayer customarily makes the rental property available during business hours for nonexclusive use by customers.
- The property is provided for use in a nonrental activity of a partnership, S corporation, or joint venture in which the taxpayer owns an interest.

Special $25,000 Allowance for Rental Real Estate
An individual who actively participates in a rental real estate activity may deduct up to $25,000 of losses from such activity against nonpassive income. This allowance is reduced by 50% of the amount by which the taxpayer's modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) exceeds $100,000, and is completely phased out when MAGI reaches $150,000.

Active participation is a less stringent standard than material participation. It requires the taxpayer to make management decisions in a significant and bona fide sense (such as approving tenants, deciding on rental terms, approving expenditures). A taxpayer must own at least 10% of the value of the activity to actively participate.

Real Estate Professionals Exception
A taxpayer who qualifies as a real estate professional is not subject to the passive activity rules for rental real estate activities in which the taxpayer materially participates. To qualify as a real estate professional:
1. More than half of the personal services the taxpayer performs in all trades or businesses during the tax year are performed in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates, AND
2. The taxpayer performs more than 750 hours of services during the tax year in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates.

Grouping Activities
Taxpayers may group one or more trade or business activities or rental activities as a single activity if they form an appropriate economic unit. Factors considered include similarities and differences in types of trades or businesses, common control, common ownership, geographic location, and interdependencies.

Passive Activity Loss Allocation Among Activities
When a taxpayer has losses from multiple passive activities, and total passive losses exceed total passive income, the net passive loss that is disallowed must be allocated among the activities with net losses. The allocation formula is:

Disallowed loss allocated to Activity X = (Net loss from Activity X / Total net losses from all passive activities with losses) x Total disallowed passive loss

For example, if a taxpayer has:
- Activity A net loss: $6,000
- Activity B net loss: $4,000
- Activity C net income: $3,000
Total passive losses = $10,000; Total passive income = $3,000; Net disallowed = $7,000
Activity A suspended = ($6,000 / $10,000) x $7,000 = $4,200
Activity B suspended = ($4,000 / $10,000) x $7,000 = $2,800

Example: Three Passive Activities with Mixed Results
Taxpayer has three passive activities:
Activity I: net loss of $5,000
Activity II: net loss of $3,000
Activity III: net loss of $2,000
Total passive losses: $10,000
Total passive income: $0
Net passive loss (disallowed): $10,000

Activity I suspended = ($5,000 / $10,000) x $10,000 = $5,000
Activity II suspended = ($3,000 / $10,000) x $10,000 = $3,000
Activity III suspended = ($2,000 / $10,000) x $10,000 = $2,000

If the taxpayer also had a passive activity with $4,000 of income:
Total passive losses: $10,000; Total passive income: $4,000; Net disallowed: $6,000
Activity I suspended = ($5,000 / $10,000) x $6,000 = $3,000; Allowed from I = $2,000
Activity II suspended = ($3,000 / $10,000) x $6,000 = $1,800; Allowed from II = $1,200
Activity III suspended = ($2,000 / $10,000) x $6,000 = $1,200; Allowed from III = $800

Net Passive Activity Income and Loss Computation for Tax Return
Step 1: Combine income and losses from all passive activities.
Step 2: If total passive income exceeds total passive losses, the net income is reported normally. The passive activity rules do not limit passive income.
Step 3: If total passive losses exceed total passive income, the excess loss is suspended (subject to the $25,000 rental allowance exception).

Treatment of Passive Income and Loss Categories on the Return
When reporting on the tax return:
- Passive income from partnerships and S corporations is reported on Schedule E.
- Passive income includes net rental income and income from passive trade or business activities.
- Taxable interest and dividends are PORTFOLIO income, NOT passive income. They are reported on Schedule B.
- Wages and salaries are ACTIVE income, NOT passive income.

Disposition of Passive Activity
When a taxpayer disposes of an entire interest in a passive activity in a fully taxable transaction to an unrelated party, all accumulated suspended passive losses from that activity become fully deductible (not just against passive income but against any income). This is the primary mechanism for unlocking suspended passive losses.

If the disposition is by gift, the suspended losses are added to the basis of the gifted interest. If by death, suspended losses up to the amount of the step-up in basis are permanently lost; any excess is deductible on the decedent's final return.

PART II: AT-RISK RULES (IRC Section 465)

Overview
The at-risk rules limit the deductible loss from an activity to the amount the taxpayer has at risk in that activity. The at-risk rules apply to individuals and to certain closely held C corporations.

Amount At Risk
A taxpayer's amount at risk in an activity includes:
1. The amount of money contributed to the activity.
2. The adjusted basis of property contributed to the activity.
3. Amounts borrowed for use in the activity if the taxpayer is personally liable for repayment (recourse debt), or if the taxpayer pledges property (other than property used in the activity) as security for the borrowed amount.
4. The taxpayer's share of qualified nonrecourse financing secured by real property used in the activity (special rule for real estate).

The amount at risk is increased by income and gains from the activity and decreased by losses allowed and by distributions or withdrawals from the activity.

Amounts NOT At Risk
A taxpayer is not at risk for:
- Nonrecourse loans used in the activity (except qualified nonrecourse financing for real estate).
- Cash, property, or borrowed amounts protected against loss by a guarantee, stop-loss agreement, or other similar arrangement.
- Amounts borrowed from a person who has an interest in the activity other than as a creditor, or from a related person who has an interest in the activity.

Qualified Nonrecourse Financing (QNF)
For activities of holding real property, a special rule allows qualified nonrecourse financing to be included in the amount at risk. Qualified nonrecourse financing is financing that meets ALL of the following requirements:
1. It is borrowed by the taxpayer in connection with the activity of holding real property.
2. It is secured by real property used in the activity.
3. No person is personally liable for repayment (it is nonrecourse).
4. It is not convertible debt.
5. It is loaned or guaranteed by a federal, state, or local government, or borrowed from a qualified person.

A "qualified person" is a person who is in the business of lending money, such as a bank, savings and loan, or insurance company. A qualified person does NOT include:
- A related person (as defined under IRC Section 465(b)(3)(C)).
- A person from whom the taxpayer acquired the property (the seller).
- A person who receives a fee from the taxpayer for the taxpayer's investment in the real property.

At-Risk Basis Computation Example
If a partner contributes $30,000 cash, the partnership borrows $100,000 on a recourse note (partner is personally liable for their share), and the partner's share of the recourse debt is $50,000, then the partner's at-risk amount is $80,000 ($30,000 cash + $50,000 share of recourse debt).

If instead the $100,000 loan is nonrecourse but is qualified nonrecourse financing secured by the real property, the partner's at-risk amount would still include their share of the QNF.

If the loan is nonrecourse and does NOT qualify as QNF (for example, it is from a related party or the seller), then the partner's at-risk amount does NOT include the partner's share of that loan.

At-Risk Basis with Multiple Debt Types Example
Suzie, a single taxpayer, is a partner in LOL partnership. She contributed $20,000 in cash. The partnership has the following liabilities:
- $60,000 recourse note from a bank (Suzie's share = $30,000)
- $40,000 nonrecourse note from the seller of the property

Suzie's at-risk amount = $20,000 (cash) + $30,000 (recourse) = $50,000
The $40,000 seller-financed nonrecourse note is NOT qualified nonrecourse financing because it is from the seller, not a qualified lender. Therefore Suzie's share of this note is NOT included in her at-risk amount.

Recapture of Previously Allowed Losses
If a taxpayer's amount at risk in an activity is reduced below zero at the end of a tax year (due to distributions, debt changes, etc.), the taxpayer must recapture (include in income) previously allowed losses to the extent the at-risk amount goes below zero.

Order of Application
The at-risk rules apply BEFORE the passive activity rules. A loss must first pass the at-risk limitation, and then it is subject to the passive activity rules.

INTERACTION BETWEEN AT-RISK AND PASSIVE ACTIVITY RULES

Step 1: Compute the loss from the activity.
Step 2: Apply the at-risk limitation (Section 465). Losses in excess of the at-risk amount are suspended under the at-risk rules.
Step 3: Apply the passive activity loss rules (Section 469) to the remaining allowable loss. If the activity is passive and there is not sufficient passive income, the loss is further suspended under the PAL rules.

COMMON EXAM COMPUTATION SCENARIOS

Scenario: Net Passive Activity Income
Charlie reports: Wages $50,000, Taxable interest $10,000, Partnership A passive income $8,000, Partnership B passive loss ($5,000), Rental real estate loss ($15,000).

Step 1: Passive income = $8,000 (Partnership A). Passive losses = $5,000 (B) + $15,000 (rental) = $20,000.
Step 2: Net passive loss = $20,000 - $8,000 = $12,000 disallowed (before $25,000 allowance).
Step 3: $25,000 rental allowance may apply if Charlie actively participates and MAGI < $150,000.
Step 4: Wages ($50,000) and taxable interest ($10,000) are NOT passive income; they do not offset passive losses.

Net passive income = $0 (there is a net passive LOSS). Passive income reported on the return = $8,000 gross passive income from Partnership A (offset by passive losses).
