Supplemental Reference: Retirement and Financial Planning Tax Rules

RETIREMENT PLAN CONTRIBUTION STRATEGIES

Key Decision Factors for Retirement Contributions
When a taxpayer is deciding between retirement savings options, the key considerations are:

1. Tax-deductible contributions reduce current taxable income (traditional 401(k), deductible traditional IRA).
2. Roth contributions provide no current deduction but offer tax-free withdrawals in retirement.
3. Employer matching is essentially free money and should generally be maximized first.
4. The tax bracket in retirement vs. the current tax bracket drives the traditional vs. Roth decision.

Optimal Strategy for Maximizing Tax Benefits
For a taxpayer with access to an employer plan with matching:
Step 1: Contribute enough to the employer plan to get the full employer match.
Step 2: If eligible and in a high tax bracket, maximize tax-deductible contributions (traditional 401(k)/403(b) up to the limit).
Step 3: Consider additional IRA contributions (traditional if deductible, Roth if income allows).
Step 4: If all tax-advantaged space is used, consider taxable investment accounts.

For a 58-year-old client planning to retire in 5 years:
- Maximize catch-up contributions (age 50+ allows additional $7,500 for 401(k) in 2025).
- Deductible IRA contributions if applicable (reduces AGI, which may also reduce taxation of Social Security benefits, Medicare premiums, etc.).
- Consider the tax-deferred growth period: even 5 years of tax-deferred growth is beneficial.
- The deduction from retirement contributions is an above-the-line deduction, directly reducing AGI.

FINANCIAL AID CONSIDERATIONS FOR EDUCATION PLANNING

FAFSA and Asset Assessment
The Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) uses a formula called the Expected Family Contribution (EFC) — now called the Student Aid Index (SAI) — to determine eligibility for need-based financial aid.

Asset Weighting on FAFSA:
- Student assets are assessed at UP TO 20% of their value.
- Parent assets are assessed at UP TO 5.64% of their value.
- The asset that is weighted MOST HEAVILY in determining a student's eligibility for financial aid is the student's OWN assets (savings accounts, investments in the student's name), because they are assessed at the highest rate (20%).
- Parent-owned 529 plans are treated as parent assets (5.64% rate).
- Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA) are NOT reported on FAFSA.
- The family's primary residence is NOT reported on FAFSA.
- Student-owned 529 plans are assessed at the student rate (20%).

Therefore, the asset type most heavily weighted (most detrimental to financial aid eligibility) is assets owned by the student.

Types of Higher Education Loans
- Federal Direct Subsidized Loans: Based on financial NEED. The government pays interest while the student is enrolled at least half-time.
- Federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans: NOT based on financial need. Available regardless of need.
- Federal Direct PLUS Loans: For parents or graduate students. Credit check required.
- Private student loans: From banks/lenders, NOT based on FAFSA.

The type of loan based on student financial need is the Federal Direct Subsidized Loan.

INVESTMENT ANALYSIS AND NET PRESENT VALUE (NPV)

Net Present Value (NPV) Computation
NPV is used to evaluate whether an investment is worthwhile. It compares the present value of future cash inflows to the initial investment cost.

NPV = Sum of [Cash flow in year t / (1 + discount rate)^t] - Initial investment

Decision rule:
- If NPV > 0: The investment earns more than the required rate of return; it is a good investment.
- If NPV = 0: The investment exactly earns the required rate of return.
- If NPV < 0: The investment earns less than the required rate of return; reject it.

Present Value Factor
The present value of $1 received in year t at discount rate r is: 1 / (1 + r)^t

Present Value of an Annuity
If the investment provides equal annual cash flows for n years:
PV of annuity = Annual cash flow x [(1 - (1 + r)^(-n)) / r]

Example NPV Computation:
Felicia is considering a project that requires a $120,000 initial investment and will generate $30,000 per year for 6 years. Her required rate of return is 10%.

PV of annuity factor for 6 years at 10% = [1 - (1.10)^(-6)] / 0.10 = [1 - 0.5645] / 0.10 = 4.3553

PV of cash inflows = $30,000 x 4.3553 = $130,659
NPV = $130,659 - $120,000 = $10,659

Since NPV > 0, the investment earns more than the 10% required rate of return and should be accepted.

Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
The IRR is the discount rate that makes NPV equal to zero. If IRR > required rate of return, the investment should be accepted.

After-Tax Cash Flows
When computing NPV for tax purposes, use after-tax cash flows:
After-tax cash flow = Pre-tax cash flow x (1 - tax rate) + (Depreciation x tax rate)

The depreciation tax shield (Depreciation x tax rate) is added back because depreciation is a non-cash expense that reduces taxes.

SOCIAL SECURITY BENEFITS TAXATION

Provisional Income Computation
Provisional income = AGI + Tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits

Taxation thresholds (single filers):
- If provisional income < $25,000: Benefits are not taxable.
- If $25,000 <= provisional income <= $34,000: Up to 50% of benefits are taxable.
- If provisional income > $34,000: Up to 85% of benefits are taxable.

Taxation thresholds (MFJ):
- If provisional income < $32,000: Benefits are not taxable.
- If $32,000 <= provisional income <= $44,000: Up to 50% of benefits are taxable.
- If provisional income > $44,000: Up to 85% of benefits are taxable.

The maximum amount of Social Security benefits subject to income tax is 85%.

SECTION 529 TO ROTH IRA ROLLOVER (SECURE 2.0)

Starting in 2024, unused 529 plan funds can be rolled into a Roth IRA for the beneficiary, subject to:
- 529 account must have been open for 15+ years.
- Annual rollover limited to Roth IRA contribution limit ($7,000 in 2025).
- Lifetime rollover limit: $35,000 per beneficiary.
- Contributions (and earnings on them) made within last 5 years are ineligible.
- Beneficiary must have earned income.
- Income limits for Roth IRA do NOT apply to this rollover.

This is considered an ADVANTAGE of 529 plans because it provides a safety valve for unused funds.
